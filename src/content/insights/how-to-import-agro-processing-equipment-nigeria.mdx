Nigeria's agro-processing sector is one of the most significant equipment import opportunities in West Africa right now. The federal government's push to process food locally rather than import it finished has created durable, growing demand for milling equipment, packaging lines, cassava and palm oil processing machinery, cold-chain systems, and post-harvest handling equipment.
European manufacturers are well positioned to supply it. But most who attempt to enter Nigeria for the first time make the same set of avoidable mistakes — and those mistakes are expensive.
This guide walks through the complete import compliance chain for regulated machinery entering Nigeria, so you know what is required, in what order, and where the real risk sits.
Why the compliance chain matters more than the commercial deal
Most European exporters focus their energy on finding the right Nigerian buyer and agreeing commercial terms. That is necessary — but it is not sufficient. Nigeria's import framework for regulated machinery involves at least six distinct compliance steps, each with its own timeline, its own responsible party, and its own failure point.
Getting the commercial terms right and then discovering — after goods are in transit — that SONCAP certification was not obtained, or that the buyer's bank rejected the Form M application, means demurrage costs, delayed revenue, and a damaged relationship. In some cases it means the goods cannot clear at all.
Understanding the chain before you commit is how you avoid that outcome.
Step 1 — Confirm your product's HS code and admissibility
Before anything else, confirm the correct Harmonised System (HS) code for your equipment. For agro-processing machinery, the relevant families are typically HS 8432 (agricultural machinery), HS 8433 (harvesting machinery), HS 8436 (other agricultural machinery), and HS 8438 (machinery for industrial food preparation).
Use the EU's Access2Markets tool or the WCO HS Nomenclature to confirm the code, then cross-check the Nigeria Customs Service current Import Prohibition List. This list is updated periodically — always use the current version, not last year's. Most standard agro-processing equipment is not prohibited, but confirming takes minutes and removes a critical uncertainty.
Also check the CBN Foreign Exchange Prohibited List separately. A product can be customs-admissible but still blocked from FX allocation — a distinction that catches many first-time exporters off guard.
Step 2 — Determine SONCAP requirements early
SONCAP — the Standards Organisation of Nigeria Conformity Assessment Programme — is mandatory for a wide range of regulated products entering Nigeria, including most electrical and mechanical machinery. It is not optional, and it cannot be obtained after the goods have shipped.
The SONCAP process involves obtaining a Product Certificate first, then a Shipment Certificate per consignment. Both are issued by SON-accredited inspection bodies — currently Cotecna, Intertek, and Bureau Veritas are the three appointed by SON.
The timeline matters: SONCAP certification currently runs four to six weeks from application. If you begin the process after the commercial contract is signed and the Form M window is already open, you are almost certainly going to miss the schedule.
The correct sequence is: confirm SONCAP applicability before finalising commercial terms, begin the certification process before the Form M is opened, and build the certification timeline into your shipment schedule.
Step 3 — The Nigerian buyer must open Form M
Form M is the Nigerian import declaration document. It is opened by the Nigerian buyer — not the exporter — through their Authorised Dealer Bank, using Nigeria's single-window trade portal. It is mandatory for any import valued above USD 1,000.
A few important details European exporters frequently miss:
Form M for machinery has a validity of 365 days — a genuine advantage that gives the buyer and the compliance chain time to move properly. It is not a rush document if started early.
The commercial invoice, packing list, and product details submitted with the Form M must exactly match the SONCAP Product Certificate. Discrepancies — even minor ones — cause rejection and require resubmission, which delays everything.
Form M cannot be opened without a valid local Nigerian marine cargo insurance policy. A foreign insurance policy will be rejected outright by the bank. The buyer must arrange local insurance before the application goes in.
Step 4 — Coordinate the shipment and Pre-Arrival Assessment Report
Once Form M is accepted and the SONCAP Shipment Certificate is in hand, the goods can ship. Upon arrival at the Nigerian port, a Pre-Arrival Assessment Report (PAAR) is generated through the Nigeria Customs Service NICIS II system. This is the document that drives the customs assessment and clearance process.
Customs duties are calculated on the CIF value of the goods. The applicable tariff rate for most agro-processing machinery under Nigeria's Common External Tariff is 5%, though specific equipment may attract different rates — confirm via Access2Markets before pricing the deal.
Port clearance at Apapa or Tin Can Island in Lagos is handled by a licensed customs clearing agent. The timeline from arrival to clearance, with documentation in order, is typically seven to fourteen days. With documentation issues, it can extend significantly and attract demurrage charges at the port.
Step 5 — The counterparty is as important as the compliance
Every step above assumes the Nigerian buyer is who they say they are, has the banking relationships to open Form M, has the cash flow to pay duties, and has the operational capacity to receive and install the equipment.
In practice, the single most common reason agro-processing equipment deals fail is not the compliance chain — it is the counterparty. A buyer who cannot open Form M because their bank relationship is insufficient, or who overstated their processing capacity, or whose facility is not ready to receive the shipment, creates delays and losses that no amount of SONCAP compliance can prevent.
Verifying the counterparty — confirming entity registration, checking trading history, calling references, confirming physical operations — is not a nice-to-have. It is the prerequisite that makes the rest of the process worth doing.
